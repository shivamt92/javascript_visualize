SIR Modelling

Epidemic models assume that the population can be classified into various compartments. Each compartment representing the state of the disease. The two major models in classical epidemiology are SIS and SIR where S, stands for susceptible (i.e., those who can contract the infection), I for Infectious (i.e., those who are contagious and can spread the infection) and R for Removed (i.e., those who have recovered from the infection). These are the simplest models and can be made more complex by adding more compartments. The complex models looking to accurately represent the graph also take into account birth rates and death rates along with mortality rate from the infection. Each compartment represents a population density which evolves overtime. Population transitions from one compartment to the other depending on the transition rates which are established as per simple differential equations.

SIR model is a three state model and used primarily for infections where the patient gains permanent immunity against the infection. Therefore, it has an additional compartment for recovered patient. Once an individual enters this compartment it cannot transition to any other compartment. The first transition occurs when a susceptible individual interacts with an infectious individual and in the process gets infected (S->I). The second transition occurs when the infectious individual recovers from the infections S->R. In an SIR process, rate of infection initially increases at an exponential rate and once a significant amount of population is infected it starts to die until there are zero infections
